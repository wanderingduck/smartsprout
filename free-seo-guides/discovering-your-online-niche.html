Ultimate Guide to Discovering Your Online Niche

Your online space is an area for your business to grow and thrive on the internet. This article explains, not only how to find your perfect niche, but how to lead, gain a network, and build community support for your business.

1. Stand for something: Make the world a better place!

  • Does your business mean something?
  • Does your business solve a problem?
  • Could this community rely on you, even if they don’t buy from you?
  • Is your business part of the problem or the solution?
  • Do you share common interests and goals with this community? 

Whatever problem you choose to solve, not everyone will thank you for solving it, but those that do will trust you, and this is the essence that will form, not only your customer base, but a community of advocates and supporters.

2. Catching the wave: Enter the market early

Many business owners are attracted to saturated markets, due to leaders in these spaces having demonstrated their success. But, these markets are tough and unforgiving, as your competitors have already established trust in that space.

3. Find a problem you can solve (that hasn’t been solved already)

Make a list of your skills and offerings

What skills do you possess or have access to that are rare? By listing these you can better analyse community problems and cross reference them with potential solutions. 


Where to find problems

The following platforms are a great source of community problems, many of which have not been solved:

  • Quora
  • Yahoo Answers
  • Google Auto-suggest
  • Answer the Public
  • Übersuggest
  • Reddit
  • Twitter
  • Facebook groups
  • Forums

 How to identify the problems that need solving the most

On the platforms mentioned above, look for view counts and the volume of comments. Ask yourself, are these commenters able to properly solve the problem, or is much of the “comment noise” resonating from those still seeking a solution?

(Optional) Install “Keywords Everywhere” (a free chrome browser extension). This will tell you the approximate search volume and potential value of your keyword in “Google”, “Answer the Public”, and “Übersuggest”.

4. Don't wast your time: Assess the competition before you commit

Before you enter a new market it is important to know who your competitors are and just as important, how strong they are. We will do a basic analysis using a free SEO tool and some advanced Google operators. This is not an exact science, but with practice you will get a feel for it.

Phrase Match: Many SEOs and content marketers look for total pages indexed for a search term. To achieve this, place your "long-tail" keyword (specific/niche phrase) in speachmarks e.g."same-day flower delivery uk", then click "search" - This asks Google to find your keyword (phrase) anywhere in any (Google indexed) page on the internet, but only if the phrase is complete.

Once the search is finished, take note of the number of results (above the listings)

Advanced Search Operators | I like to take this a step further using "advanced search operators" to separate accidental competing pages from targeted pages. This will give you a more accurate understanding of the ranking difficulty attached to each phrase.

allinurl: asks for pages that contain all of the specified words in the url

allintext: requests that all of the words are found in the content of the page.

allintitle: calls for pages that contain all the specified words in the title

Using "quotes" | also placing quotes or speech marks around your phrase calls only pages where the phrase is complete and in order.

Times are changing and so is Google. Google used to be rank pages specifically based on keywords (in content,in URL,in title, and in the description), but Google is now capable of "understanding" topics and themes.

This means they are more interested in the theme of a page than the specific words used, thus it is no longer necessary for your competition to use these words to rank for your chosen search term.

Competitor strength | Install the free Mozbar extension on your chrome browser. This will give you insight into the "authority"of your competitors pages.

Search again for your phrases without quotes or search operators (a broad match search) and take note of both the "Page Authority" (PA) and "Domain Authority" (DA) of the pages ranking on the first page of Google.

If all of the ranking pages look targeted (for your phrase or theme) and have high "Page Authority" and/or "Domain Authority", You may find it difficult to rank, even if the volume of results is low.

However, if some of the pages appear to be there accidentally, you may be in with a chance.

Though the techniques demonstrated above will only estimate your competition, it is still useful, as digital marketers and SEOs are still using keywords to rank their pages.
Which problem/solution should you pursue?

When you have a list of problems you can solve, or contribute to; place them in a spreadsheet with the following headers and populate the columns:

  • Problem
  • Intensity of need (1-10)
  • Potential to contribute (1-10)
  • Competition (allinurl:)
  • Competition (allintitle:)

Now cross-reference the highest “intensity of need” with your “potential to contribute” (you want high numbers in both columns). Then ask yourself “Can I solve multiple problems in this space at the same time?”

It may be helpful to add a "conditional formatting" colour scale to the competition values to gain a better perspective of the difficulty for each term/topic.

5. Conquer the niche: Be the best in your space

Be the big fish in a small space, then expand your area. Your potential customers will follow those with the strength to lead.

Facebook is a good example of this. Facebook was originally built to be used in one single university. Soon after the students accepted this tool, it was offered to students of multiple educational institutions. It remained exclusive for some time, only allowing members who had college/university email addresses to create an account.

This was soon adopted all over the world by students, and Facebook subsequently opened to the whole population, and for the majority, Myspace was abandoned as the social network of choice.

Google+, however was a different story. Google tried to build a better social network, but instead of looking to solve a problem, they attempted to compete directly with Facebook. Although there are many people using Google+ today (mainly in the USA), most were not convinced by the new social network.

Other platforms such as LinkedIn, Twitter, Snapchat, Reddit, and Instagram are thriving, as each has chosen its own space, allowed other platforms to co-exist, and solved a unique problem.

6. Dominate your space: Become the authority in your niche

One thing that all your customers have in common is trust. They trust that the product/service you offer is right for them, right now, and that they have the best possible solution.

So, it’s no surprise that when business slips through your fingers it is due to a lack of trust in you and/or your service to meet their needs.

7. Help the community: Build trust in your online space

You probably went into business to make money, but if you want to be successful in your space, you must make friends and gain the trust of your potential market segment.

If the best choices in the market aren’t obvious, those in your market will look for a “tour guide”.

If you share the interests and common goals of the community you wish to serve, then for the most part, serve the community without selling. Offer solutions to problems and develop your personal and business image/voice as trustworthy and dependable. Invest in the community and be seen everywhere.

8. Build a network: Offer to support those who serve your market

What skills do you have that set you apart? Volunteer those skills to serve and support those with authority and trust in your niche.


Where to find influencers/leaders in your space:

  • LinkedIn
  • Twitter
  • Facebook groups
  • Quora
  • Reddit
  • Forums

Is there a resource that connects you? If not, consider building one!

Offer to help those who already solve problems in your space. Allow their motives to co-exist with yours, rather than becoming their competition; and they will support you, guide you, and offer their skills and connections to help develop you and your business.

9. Hold out your hands: Be there for your market

Don’t just aim to network on the platforms above, choose your weapon, engage and join the conversation, offer solutions, ask questions and support others.

I don’t suggest you try to dominate all these platforms as you will spread yourself too thin and become ineffective.

Once you have chosen your platforms, dedicate time each day to answering questions, writing helpful posts and serving your community.

10. Don’t stop exploring: Learn the landscape and adapt

Once you have started to solve problems in your space, other issues will become apparent. To maximise your impact, without running out of time, look for ways to solve multiple problems at once. This way, you will be more effective and attract a wider audience.

11. Trust is a long game: Build a long-term strategy that works

Before you begin your journey, build a long-term plan. If you improvise each day and fail to plan you will become lost and demotivated. Be sure that your activities are all part of a bigger plan and when something puts you off track, make sure you have a contingency built into your long-term strategy to make up for it.

12. Don’t cash in early: Invest your time to reap rewards

If you are looking for a “quick win”, this guide will not help you, as your lack of dedication to your market will become apparent. Building a long-term strategy and adapting over time will bring your business goals to life.

Over several months of continuously serving this niche community, you will experience compound rewards.

As you build trust, you will build supporters, and as you are revered as an expert, your business will begin to dominate your online space.
